Statement of Findings

The Amazon is a rainforest that spans 6.7 million square kilometres. It is currently the largest rainforest biome on the Earth and is twice the size of India (Rainforest Cruises, 2017, WWF, 2005). It also contains the Amazon river, which flows for more than 6,600km and counts for 15-16% of the world's total river discharge into the ocean.

Species in the Amazon

The massive rainforest itself contains 390 billion individual trees and 16,000 different species of tree (Eureka Alert, 2013). It contains roughly 13% of the 3 trillion trees the Earth houses (Geography Realm, 2015). It also has other biomes, like savanna, floodplain forests, grasslands, swamps, bamboos and palm forests. (Mongabay, 2019)

Location of Biome

The Amazon spans over 8 different countries and 6.7 million square kilometres, but the forest is mostly in Brazil and Peru. Brazil takes 64% coverage, Peru takes 10%, Columbia takes 6% and Bolivia takes 6% of coverage of the Amazon rainforest, with the other percentage being other countries (World Atlas, 2017). Usually tourists who want to explore the rainforest will base themselves in Brazil, Peru, Bolivia and Ecuador, as you are only permitted entry into the forest with a tour guide (Rainforest Cruises, 2017).

Rainforest climate

The Amazon rainforest is also very close to the equator, making the rainforest climate hot and damp (Active Wild, 2015). Other significant rainforests around the world is the Congo Rainforest in central Africa and the Valdivian Temperate Rainforest on the west coast of southern South America. (Conserve Energy Future, 2019)

Significant Threats and Conservation Efforts

The Amazon rainforest is currently under threat because of deforestation and multiple wildfires happening within the forest. Since the 1950s, 17% of the Amazon's trees have been cut down to clear land for more livestock and other properties (World Bank, 2013). The wildfires have also been more prominent in the recent years, and recently there were an outburst of even more fires. This is a problem because the Amazon rainforest provides over 20% of the Earth’s total oxygen (Newsweek, 2019).

At one point, more than 40,000 fires were burning inside the Amazon. They were mostly all manmade fires and began spreading from there, as the reason these fires started is because of bad farming practices. This is done regularly to burn through newly felled forests that have been cleared to make way for pastures and burning them boosts the nutrients in the soil (ABC News, 2019). This cycle is alarming scientists, because the forest is close to an irreversible state (The Guardian, 2019, National Geographic, 2019). It also is destroying many animal habitats and destroying plant life. Consequences for loss of habitat can include endangerment or extinction of a species.

Recommendations

ARPA

In 2002, Brazil launched the biggest forest conservation effort in history, known as the Amazon Region Protected Areas (ARPA). The goal is to take hundreds of millions of acres of the Amazon and turn it into sustained-use and strict protected areas, which is a huge task. In over a decade, the ARPA has protected a California-sized area of the Amazon already. The ARPA’s conservation efforts have helped dramatically with deforestation, and they are looking to provide ongoing protection for the 30 million people living in the Amazon as well as all of the different species of animal and plant. (WWF, 2014)

One Tree Planted

One Tree Planted is an organisation that started in 2014, focusing on planting trees in many locations including the Amazon Rainforest. According to Taviana MacLeod, an employee from One Tree Planted, they would have planted close to 700,000 trees in the Amazon (Refer to Appendix 3). The reforestation project is located in the Madre de Dios region in Peru. Hosting a population of over 140,000 (citypopulation.de, 2017), it is very dense with biodiversity, as it is home to never-ending forests, winding rivers and abundant wildlife (Peru Travel, 2014). Planting more trees increase oxygen levels, give animals more opportunities for habitats and slowly decrease the climate change epidemic that is ongoing.

Chosen Recommendation

The best solution to this problem is to plant more trees, as we are losing so many trees across all of the world’s forests. One Tree Planted is one of the many companies/charities that plants trees around the globe. The popular “Team Trees” collaboration between many YouTubers, started by YouTubers MrBeast and Mark Rober, with their mission to plant 20,000,000 trees.


Team Trees partnered with the Arbor Day Foundation to receive help with planting trees, as well as make sure they are being planted well and to assure they grow healthy (Team Trees/Arbor Day Foundation, 2019).

Planting and regrowing more trees ensure we can replenish the areas that have been affected by deforestation in many forests and parts of the world. Also, regrowing trees will heavily reduce, and if not, completely eliminate the climate change crisis, as the globe is warming, and many greenhouse gasses are being emitted into our atmosphere. The more gas that escapes to our atmosphere, the ozone layers will reduce and heat the globe by the smallest amount. This will cause even hotter summers, rising ocean levels, droughts, as well as wipe out coral reefs and melt more ice caps. Scientists say we have ten years to solve this climate change crisis, before we cannot go back (CNN, 2018). Growing more trees are the way to go, and if the whole world gets involved, we can properly address the climate problem in a couple of years.
